Research Context

The invisible financial problem

Uzbekistan receives 14.4% of its GDP in remittances — one of the highest ratios in the world, and a figure that masks a deeper structural reality. According to World Bank analysis, without these transfers the national poverty rate would be 16.8% rather than 9.6%. Remittances are not a supplement to the Uzbek economy. They are a pillar of it.

Yet the country's financial tools — budgeting apps, loan calculators, savings guides — almost uniformly ignore this reality. They assume salary income in UZS, local spending only, and no currency exposure. The specific financial decisions facing remittance households remain entirely unmapped: which corridor to use, when to convert RUB to UZS, how to plan around ruble volatility, whether to save in USD or soms.

The 2022 ruble shock illustrated the stakes precisely. When sanctions caused the ruble to lose 25% of its dollar value, remittance income from Russia fell by an estimated 21%. Around 133,000 Uzbek migrants returned home in Q1 2022 alone. The financial fragility of remittance-dependent households became visible in a crisis — but no tool had been built to help them manage it before one.

SilkEcon is an attempt to make this invisible financial decision-space legible at the household level. The tools here model the specific constraints and opportunities of Uzbek families with income abroad, drawing on Central Bank, World Bank, and IOM data. It is also an open research question: what is the household-level welfare cost of remittance corridor fees and currency risk? The data exists to estimate it.
